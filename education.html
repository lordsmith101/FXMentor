What is Forex Trading?

The foreign exchange market (Forex) is where currencies are traded. It's the largest financial market in the world, with over $6 trillion traded daily. Forex trading involves buying one currency while simultaneously selling another.

Key Characteristics

  • 24-hour market (except weekends)
  • High liquidity (easy to enter/exit trades)
  • Low transaction costs compared to other markets
  • Ability to profit in both rising and falling markets

Essential Forex Terms

Pip

A pip (percentage in point) is the smallest price move that a currency pair can make. For most pairs, it's 0.0001 (1/100th of 1%). Example: If EUR/USD moves from 1.1050 to 1.1051, that's 1 pip.

Example: USD/JPY at 109.85 moving to 109.86 = +1 pip

Lot

A lot is the standardized quantity of a currency.

  • Standard lot = 100,000 units
  • Mini lot = 10,000 units
  • Micro lot = 1,000 units

Example: 1 standard lot of EUR/USD = €100,000

Leverage

Leverage allows traders to control large positions with relatively little capital. Expressed as a ratio (e.g., 50:1, 100:1, 500:1). While it can amplify profits, it also increases potential losses.

Example: With 100:1 leverage, $1,000 controls $100,000 position

Currency Pairs

Currencies are traded in pairs (e.g., EUR/USD). The first currency is the base, the second is the quote. Major pairs include EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY, USD/CHF, AUD/USD, USD/CAD, NZD/USD.

Example: EUR/USD = 1.1200 means 1 Euro = 1.12 US Dollars

Spread

The spread is the difference between the bid (sell) and ask (buy) price. This is how brokers make money. Lower spreads are better for traders as they reduce trading costs.

Example: EUR/USD bid=1.1200, ask=1.1202 → spread=2 pips

Margin

Margin is the amount of money required to open and maintain a leveraged position. It's a good faith deposit, not a fee. Margin requirements vary by broker and leverage.

Example: 1% margin on $100,000 position = $1,000 required

How Forex Trading Works

Buying and Selling Currencies

When you trade forex, you're essentially buying one currency while selling another. If you think the base currency will strengthen against the quote currency, you buy (go long). If you think it will weaken, you sell (go short).

Remember

All trades involve two currencies - you're always long one and short the other.

Basic Trading Example

Scenario: You believe the Euro will rise against the US Dollar.
Action: Buy EUR/USD at 1.1200
Outcome A (Profit): Price rises to 1.1250 (+50 pips)
Outcome B (Loss): Price falls to 1.1150 (-50 pips)
Lot Size: 1 standard lot (€100,000)
Pip Value: $10 per pip (for standard lots)
